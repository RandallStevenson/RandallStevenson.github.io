Statistical Method #1



We will test to see if there is a signficant difference in the temperatures within and beyond 45 degrees latitude from the equator.

Hypothesis: The temperature more than 45 degrees latitude from the equator is less than the temperature within 45 degrees latitude of the equator. μ 45+ is less than μ 45-
Null Hypothesis: Temperature more than 45 degrees latitude from the equator is greater than or equal to the temperature within 45 degrees latitude of the equator. μ 45+ ≥ μ 45-
Significance Level: 1%.
Test Method: One-tailed, two-sample t-test

Statistical Method #2



We will test the fit of temperature as a function of cosine of latitude to temperture being a constant.

Hypothesis: The temperature is a function of latitude approximated by -44.7+125.3×cos(lat). Alternative Hypothesis: The temperature is independent of the cosine of the latitude. Significance level: 1% Test Method: Fisher’s F-test Statistical Analysis: Let y = -44.7+125.3×cos(lat)
SSEx (sum of squares of errors for constant assumption) =896866.5
SSEy (sum of squares of errors for ys) = 90346.93
DF = 564 (use 120 in the F-table, for conservatism)

Test Statistic: F=(SSEx – SSEy)/( SSEy/564) = 5034.7
Interpretation: To pass the F-test at a 1% significance, F must be greater than 1.533, which it does. Therefore, we are at least 99% certain the model -44.7 + 125.3 x cos(latitude) for temperature is more accurate than assuming the temperature is 53.8 regardless of latitude. [Note: By further F-tests, a linear regression to the absolute value of the latitude also passes the F-test at 99% certainty,
but the cosine model is at least 99% likely to be a better model than the linear regression model.]

CONCLUSION

The temperature on the earth decreases as the distance from the equator increases. The average temperature (°F) at a latitude can be approximated by -44.7 + 125.3 × cos(latitude).
